Brown v. Board of Education

On May 17th, 1954, the Supreme Court passed the landmark decision in the Brown v. Board of Education of Topeka case. The ruling declared that segregation in public schools was unconstitutional because it conflicted with the 14th Amendment which stated that all races have equal protection under the law. Chief Justice Earl Warren abolished the "separate but equal" doctrine that had stood for over 50 years as a result of the Plessy v. Ferguson case. This case was by far one of the biggest advancements by the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP) and a step forward in gradually abolishing the horrible Jim Crow Laws.

Rosa Parks and the Montgomery Bus Boycott

The Montgomery Bus Boycott occurred in Montgomery, Alabama from December of 1955 to December of 1956. Following the incident in which Rosa Parks refused to surrender her seat on the public bus to a white man, several civil rights groups including the NAACP rose to action by proclaiming that no person of color would use public transportation in the city. The work of this movement eventually led to a Supreme Court ruling which stated segregation on public buses was unconstitutional. This event highlighted a greater awareness by the colored community in organizing large-scale events to limit discrimination.

Southern Christian Leadership Conference

In early 1957, the Southern Christian Leadership Conference (SCLC) was founded by none other than Dr. Martin Luther King Junior and some other activists. The SCLC was one of the main civil rights organizations that advocated against segregation in American society and was a promoter of events such as the March on Washington in 1963. Today, they continue to harbor advancements in underrepresented Black neighborhoods. The SCLC is known for pioneering nonviolence as a defining factor of the civil rights movement.

March on Washington

On August 28, 1963, a myriad of civil rights leaders organized one of the largest African American movements to date–the March on Washington. This massive protest garnered an estimated 250,000 people who were witness to iconic moments including MLK’s “I Have a Dream” speech. This march was a pivotal moment in Black civil rights because it largely contributed to the Civil Rights Act of 1964 which legally banned any form of discrimination on the basis of race, sex, and other natural measures.

Black Panthers Civil Rights Group

Founded in 1966, the Black Panthers Party for Self-Defense was an organization that used force by Black Americans to fight back against policy brutality and other forms of targetted violent racism. They hosted a variety of programs to educate the Black community as well as provided humanitarian services to them. The Black Panthers differed from the established civil rights groups in that they believed the movement was not progressing fast enough along with being the main party that used violence where needed. However, after they came under government repression, they declined and sparsely existed in the 1970s.

Malcolm X

A forefront figure in the American Civil Rights Movement, Malcolm Little, also known by his alias Malcolm X, began his advocacy as part of the Nation of Islam. The Nation of Islam was a Black separatist group which encouraged publicity of black pride/power and discontent with the speed of nonviolent progress in Black Civil Rights. Malcolm X was a fiery speaker who argued for a rather radical approach to quelling social discrimination. Unfortunately, his controversial views made some in the public have strong views about him which eventually led to his assassination in 1965.

Martin Luther King Jr.

Dr. Martin Luther King Junior (Dr. MLK Jr.) is widely recognized as the most prominent figure in the African American Civil Rights Movement. He is popularly known for applying Mahatma Gandhi’s ideals of nonviolent protests coupled with his moving speeches in his efforts to decrease social injustices. MLK rose to popularity by spearheading the Montgomery Bus Boycott and delivering his famous “I Have a Dream” speech in which he detailed what he hoped and aimed for America to be for Black posterity. Despite his assassination in 1968, his legacy lives on as a worldwide champion for the nonviolent fight towards racial equality.
